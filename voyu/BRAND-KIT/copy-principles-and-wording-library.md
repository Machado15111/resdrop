## PART 1 — COPY PRINCIPLES

### The 8 Laws of Voyu Copy:

**1. Desire before information.**
Make the reader feel something before you tell them anything. The emotional pull comes first — then the facts confirm the decision.

**2. Specific beats general, always.**
"A suite that wakes up facing the Quadrado in Trancoso" beats "luxury villa in Bahia." Specificity creates desire. Generality creates skepticism.

**3. One thought per sentence.**
Voyu copy is architectural — clean lines, no clutter. Short sentences. White space between ideas.

**4. Let the place do the talking.**
Describe what the traveler will *experience*, not what the hotel *has*. Not "the hotel has a pool." Instead: "You'll spend mornings in the pool watching the jungle move."

**5. Confidence without superlatives.**
Never say "best" or "world-class" or "the most amazing." Say what it is. Confidence comes from specificity, not from claim.

**6. Warmth is a strategic choice.**
This brand should feel like a trusted friend who knows Brazil inside out. Not a company. Not a corporation. A curator who genuinely cares.

**7. Never justify price.**
The moment you talk about price in a creative, you've made price the subject. Price is never the subject. *The experience is the subject.*

**8. The CTA is an invitation, not a command.**
"Book Now" feels like a push. "Plan your stay" feels like an opening. Lead with service, not urgency.

## PART 2 — HEADLINE STYLE

### Headline Anatomy:

Great Voyu headlines do one of three things:
- **Create a picture** → "Waking up in Trancoso with nothing between you and the ocean."
- **Reframe Brazil** → "Brazil has a version most travelers never find. We know where it is."
- **Offer a promise** → "Your stay in Rio, exactly as it should be."

### Headline Formulas:

**Formula A — The Evocative Scene:**
> "[Sensory action] in [specific place]."
- "Waking up in Trancoso with the Quadrado just outside your door."
- "Dinner under fig trees at a pousada most travelers walk past."
- "The morning light in your villa in Bahia, filtered through banana leaves."

**Formula B — The Reframe:**
> "Brazil has a [X] most travelers never find. [Promise/resolution]."
- "Brazil has a quiet side most tourists never reach. We'll show you where."
- "There's a version of Rio that doesn't feel like Rio. It feels like home."
- "The best place to stay in Florianópolis isn't the most obvious one."

**Formula C — The Direct Curation:**
> "The [adjective] [hotel/villa/stay] in [destination], curated."
- "The most considered boutique hotel in Santa Teresa."
- "A private villa in Trancoso for people who know what they want."
- "Rio's most quietly spectacular hotel — for those who've already seen the obvious ones."

**Formula D — The Invitation:**
> "[Destination] is waiting. [What Voyu does]."
- "Bahia is waiting. Let us handle exactly where you stay."
- "You've already decided on Brazil. Now decide where to wake up."
- "Fernando de Noronha, done right."

**Formula E — The Trust Signal:**
> "We've stayed there. We've walked the property. [Confidence statement]."
- "We've stayed in over 40 properties across Brazil. This is one we keep recommending."
- "Not every hotel in Rio is worth your time. This one is."

## PART 3 — CAPTION STYLE

### Caption Structure (3-Part Model):

**Open → Develop → Invite**

```
[Open: 1–2 evocative sentences that create desire]

[Develop: 2–4 sentences of context, curation, specific detail]

[Invite: 1 soft CTA — never pushy]
```

### Caption Length Guide:
- **Discovery posts (feed):** 60–150 words — enough to create desire, short enough to not overstay
- **Curation posts (carousels):** 100–200 words — first caption is the hook, subsequent slides have shorter captions
- **Story captions:** 0–20 words — let the image carry the weight
- **Ad copy (primary text):** 50–120 words — desire + trust + CTA

### Caption Tone Calibration by Audience:

**For American travelers:**
- More direct, slightly more informational
- Comparison references they recognize (Tulum, Bali, Amalfi)
- Ease and logistics matter: "we handle everything"
- Safety/trust signals land well

**For European travelers:**
- More atmospheric, editorial, design-led
- Appreciate cultural depth and authenticity
- References to architecture, food, art
- Less "everything is easy" — more "you've discovered something most miss"

## PART 4 — CTA STYLE

### CTA Hierarchy (from softest to most direct):

**Tier 1 — Discovery (top of funnel):**
- "Explore where to stay in Brazil →"
- "See our curated properties →"
- "Discover Brazil through the right stay →"
- "Follow for curated stays in Brazil"

**Tier 2 — Consideration (warm audience):**
- "Tell us where you're dreaming of going — we'll handle the rest."
- "Planning a trip to Brazil? Let's talk about where you'll stay."
- "DM us your destination. We'll send you our top picks."
- "Thinking about Brazil? Start with where to stay."

**Tier 3 — Inquiry (ready to convert):**
- "Ready to plan your stay? Link in bio."
- "Plan your Brazil stay with us → voyutravel.com"
- "Reach out — we'll find the right place for you."
- "This property is available. Let's talk dates."

**What to never use:**
```
❌ "Book now!"
❌ "Limited availability — act fast!"
❌ "Don't miss out"
❌ "Best price guaranteed"
❌ "Click here"
❌ "Call us today"
❌ "Hurry"
```

## PART 6 — TRUST WORDING LIBRARY

**For service trust:**
- "We've stayed there."
- "Our team has walked every property we recommend."
- "We only suggest what we'd choose ourselves."
- "If we wouldn't stay there, we won't recommend it."
- "Every property in our collection has been personally assessed."
- "We work with [X] properties across Brazil."
- "We handle the research, the reservation, and the details."

**For Brazil trust (addressing fears):**

- "We know the properties that deliver — and the ones that don't."
- "The right stay changes everything."
- "You'll arrive knowing exactly what to expect."

- "We're here before, during, and after your stay."

## PART 7 — HOTEL DESIRE WORDING LIBRARY

Use these phrases when describing hotels:

**For boutique hotels:**
- "The kind of hotel that feels like it was made for someone exactly like you."
- "Not the largest hotel in [city]. The most considered."
- "Boutique doesn't mean small. It means deliberate."
- "A property where every room tells a different story."
- "Designed to disappear into its surroundings — and deliver something extraordinary."

**For iconic/flagship hotels:**
- "If you're going to do Rio, this is how you do it."
- "Some hotels are famous for a reason. This is one of them."
- "The view from this terrace has converted skeptics about [destination] for decades."
- "The kind of address that stays with you."
- "An icon that earns it."

**For design hotels:**
- "Architecture that makes the destination feel more itself."
- "Every detail chosen with intention."
- "The kind of place you photograph for yourself, not for anyone else."
- "Design that enhances the experience rather than performing it."

## PART 8 — VILLA DESIRE WORDING LIBRARY

Use these phrases when describing villas:

**For privacy/space:**
- "Your own corner of Brazil."
- "A house with no lobby, no queue, no shared pool."
- "The kind of privacy that changes the pace of a trip."
- "Wake up on your own terms. No schedule, no strangers."
- "A villa is a different category of stay. Not just more space — a different life."

**For groups and families:**
- "Room for everyone. Privacy for you."
- "A house where everyone arrives and nobody wants to leave."
- "Designed for the trip you actually want — not the one that fits in a hotel corridor."

**For romance/couples:**
- "A villa in Trancoso for two, with nothing between you and what matters."
- "Private pool. Private view. Private Brazil."
- "The most romantic stays in Brazil don't have a reception desk."

**For general villa desire:**
- "A home in Brazil, for however long you want it."
- "The villa didn't come with a concierge. It came with a curator: us."
- "Villas we've chosen because the photos don't tell the whole story — but we know the whole story."

## PART 9 — BRAZIL POSITIONING WORDING LIBRARY

### Repositioning Brazil for international travelers:

**The comparison play (when targeting people considering other destinations):**
- "You've thought about Ibiza. You've thought about Tulum. Have you thought about Brazil?"
- "Everything you want from the Mediterranean — warm water, great food, beautiful design — with a warmth that's entirely its own."
- "St. Barths has the luxury. Brazil has the luxury and the soul."
- "Tulum without the crowds. Ibiza without the noise. Bahia."
- "The South of France had its moment. Brazil is having its moment now."

**The discovery play (for travelers who already know Brazil):**
- "There's a version of Brazil most first-timers never reach. These are the stays that get you there."
- "Not just a beach destination. A country with extraordinary design, food, and hospitality."
- "Brazil isn't one place. Here's how to choose the right corner of it."

**The beauty play:**
- "Brazil is unexpectedly — and relentlessly — beautiful."
- "There's a reason people who visit once come back every year."
- "The water in Maragogi is the kind of blue that makes you re-examine every other destination."
- "Fernando de Noronha has no cars, no crowds, and water that belongs in a different universe."
- "Rio from a hotel terrace is one of the great views in the world. Full stop."

## PART 10 — WHAT NOT TO SAY

| Never use | Reason |
|-----------|--------|
| "Paradise" | Generic, overused, meaningless |
| "World-class" | Empty superlative |
| "Luxury experience" | Vague, self-congratulatory |
| "Affordable luxury" | Conflates value with price |
| "Best deal" / "great price" | Never make price the subject |
| "Dream vacation" | Cliché, tourist-brochure language |
| "Stunning" | Overused, lacks specificity |
| "Amazing" | Too casual, not refined |
| "Don't miss" / "Must-see" | Tourist-guide language |
| "Book now — limited availability" | Creates pressure, not desire |
| "All-inclusive" | Wrong tier positioning |
| "Package" | Implies generic bundling |
| "Tour" | Wrong category |
| "Cheap" / "Budget-friendly" | Off-brand entirely |
